When Lots Matter: The Impact of SEC Round Lot Modernization on Liquidity

Preston Mantel

  • Examines how SEC round lot modernization changes displayed and realized trading costs.
  • Separates the visibility, crumbling-quote, and adverse-selection channels behind the reform.
  • Documents a large gap between headline spread improvements and savings realized by investors.
View paper
Read abstract

When a transparency reform improves headline market quality metrics, how much of the improvement reaches investors? I provide the first empirical analysis of the SEC's round lot modernization, which dynamically adjusts round lot sizes by price tier and lets smaller orders in high-priced stocks set the NBBO for the first time. Conducting an event study around the November 2025 compliance date, I document a sharp wedge between displayed and realized benefits: quoted spreads narrow by 18.4%, implying $10.5 billion in annual savings if every trade executed at the quote, but effective spreads narrow by only 6.7%, delivering $1.4 billion in realized savings. The seven-fold gap indicates that the reform primarily redistributes trade execution within the spread rather than reducing trading costs dollar-for-dollar. To explain the wedge, I disentangle two channels of inside-the-quote activity. The mechanical channel, crumbling quotes, significantly amplifies welfare gains, while the information channel, adverse selection, is insignificant for spread outcomes. The reform succeeds by making residual quotes visible, not by resolving information asymmetry, and ETFs, which face the same lot reduction without meaningful adverse selection or crumbling-quote exposure, replicate the visibility effects without the welfare gains. The reform also alters optimal stock split design by making post-split liquidity sensitive to round lot reassessment timing.

Democratizing or Demoralizing: The Impact of Robinhood's Order Types on Retail Trading Costs

Preston Mantel with Mehmet Sağlam

  • Studies how Robinhood's order-collaring policy affects retail execution costs.
  • Combines regular-hours, after-hours, and SEC Rule 605 evidence.
  • Identifies the broker incentives and investor costs created by the policy.
View paper
Read abstract

Order collaring at Robinhood automatically converts default market orders into limit orders with a 5% spread over prior prices, advertised as protecting retail investors from unfavorable execution. However, we provide empirical evidence that this policy harms retail traders by increasing trading costs while generating outsized broker revenue. During regular trading hours, collared orders execute at an additional spread of 1.9 basis points compared to uncollared sell orders on the same stocks. In after-hours trading, a significant share of orders execute at the full 5% spread. Using SEC Rule 605 disclosures, we find that Robinhood's marketable limit orders face 1.3 basis points higher effective spreads than market orders, a 15% increase in trading costs. Analysis of payment-for-order-flow revenues reveals that Robinhood's collared orders generate 11 to 13 cents per share in additional broker revenue, a 21% premium relative to standard market orders from other brokers, revealing the financial incentive behind the policy. Aggregate retail losses reach up to $35 million per month. We establish causality through quasi-experiments exploiting Robinhood's asymmetric buy and sell collaring, the sharp 5% discontinuity in after-hours trading, its after-hours extension in 2022, and its 2025 policy removal. Our analysis of the GameStop episode further reveals that order collaring amplifies extreme price movements in high-retail stocks.

Taker Delays and Liquidity Provision in Prediction Markets

Preston Mantel

  • Studies how taker delays protect resting orders in short-duration prediction markets.
  • Synchronizes Polymarket order books with Binance data and on-chain settlement records.
  • Finds broader maker participation without a measurable loss in forecast accuracy.
Draft available upon request
Read abstract

I study what happens when a market gives resting orders latency protection against traders who react to a fully public payoff signal. Polymarket introduced a 500-millisecond taker delay on 15-minute and 1-hour crypto Up or Down contracts on November 14, 2025, leaving longer-duration crypto contracts on the same underlyings untreated. Synchronizing the Polymarket order book with Binance tick data, I show the protection worked at the mechanism level: after sharp external price jumps, the probability that a stale resting quote is hit within two seconds falls by 9 percentage points relative to within-crypto controls, and the fast aggressive traders who had been earning 1.8 cents per take see that edge compressed by roughly a quarter without reducing their activity. The rents were competed away through entry, which is the paper's central causal result: treated markets gain 21.0 maker addresses per market and log maker count rises by 0.28, with clean pre-trends. On-chain settlement records show 105,474 new maker addresses entering during the protected regime and supplying 59% of its maker volume, while average per-fill maker profitability is unchanged, consistent with free entry. Turnover is extreme, and 81% of delay-era addresses exit within three months of the delay's removal. Forecast accuracy is unchanged. Latency protection reallocated rents from fast takers to a broader, largely transient population of liquidity suppliers without degrading information aggregation.

Discriminatory Market Making: Evidence from Institutional Trades

Preston Mantel with Mehmet Sağlam

  • Tests whether market makers price institutional clients according to inferred informativeness.
  • Finds no broad discrimination against informed clients across execution-quality measures.
  • Documents bilateral learning from a market maker's own profit-and-loss history with each client.
Draft available upon request
Read abstract

We test whether market makers learn about and discriminate against informed institutional clients. Using proprietary data on institutional parent and child orders spanning January 2011 to March 2012, we construct a strictly backward-looking measure of client informativeness based on lagged cumulative price impact. Despite high persistence in client informativeness, we find no evidence that market makers discriminate against informed clients. The interaction between market maker fills and client informativeness is statistically insignificant across price-impact horizons, for both effective spreads and price improvement. This null result holds in both in-sample and walk-forward out-of-sample classifications, despite evidence that informed clients impose real costs on market makers. However, we document that market makers do learn from bilateral experience. When an individual market maker has lost money filling a specific client, it subsequently charges wider effective spreads and provides less price improvement. This bilateral learning is asymmetric, with losses having approximately three times the impact of gains, and is purely bilateral. A market maker's pricing responds to its own experience with a client but not to other market makers' experience with the same client. The bilateral adjustment does not depend on whether the client is classified as informed, suggesting that market makers respond to their own profit and loss rather than to client informativeness itself. Clients respond strategically, routing significantly more volume to market makers that offered better prices, creating a two-sided equilibrium.
